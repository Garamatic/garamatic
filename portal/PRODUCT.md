# Product

## Register

product

## Users

Citizens of Desgoffe, a French-speaking municipality. They use this portal to submit municipal requests (nuisance reports, permits, general inquiries) and track their progress. They are not necessarily tech-savvy; they expect government services to be clear, reliable, and easy to navigate. The primary task is either filing a new request or checking the status of an existing one.

## Product Purpose

Guichet Citoyen is the digital front desk for municipal services. It replaces or supplements in-person visits with an online channel that is transparent, fast, and accessible. Success is measured by citizen satisfaction, reduced physical queue load, and request resolution time — not by engagement metrics or time-on-site.

## Brand Personality

Proud, local, distinctive.

- **Proud**: The town of Desgoffe has its own identity. The interface should carry that identity with confidence, not apologize for being a municipal site.
- **Local**: Citizens should feel they are talking to *their* town hall, not a generic SaaS platform. The personality should be warm enough to be approachable, but not so casual that it undermines the authority of the service.
- **Distinctive**: The design should not be interchangeable with any other municipality's portal. It should feel like Desgoffe, not a white-label template.

## Anti-references

- Generic white-label government portals that look like they could be any town.
- Corporate SaaS dashboards with neon accents, hero metrics, and "streamline / empower" marketing copy.
- Playful startup aesthetics: cartoon illustrations, bouncy animations, bright gradients.
- Rigid 1990s institutional government sites: all-gray, cluttered tables, paper-form metaphors.

## Design Principles

1. **Authority without coldness**: The interface should feel official enough to trust, but not so cold that citizens feel alienated. The town's identity is the bridge between the two.
2. **Show the citizen, not the system**: The dashboard should surface what a citizen needs to know (status, next steps, timeline), not internal system fields or technical jargon.
3. **Progress over state**: Wherever possible, show what is *happening* next, not just what the current label is. A status is more useful when it comes with a human-readable explanation and an expected timeline.
4. **One path, no dead ends**: Every screen should have a clear next action. If a citizen lands on the dashboard with no requests, the empty state should guide them to submit one. If a ticket is closed, the success page should explain how to follow up.
5. **Respect the citizen's time**: Municipal requests are chores, not entertainment. The interface should be fast to navigate, quick to fill, and easy to leave. No dark patterns, no unnecessary friction, no "stay on the site" nudges.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

- WCAG 2.1 AA compliance is the baseline for all screens.
- Keyboard navigation and screen reader support are required for all interactive elements.
- Color contrast must meet AA standards for all text and UI components.
- Reduced motion support: all animations should respect `prefers-reduced-motion`.
- The interface is in French; i18n-ready structure should be maintained for future language support.
